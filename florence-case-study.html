Data Storytelling

A Year of Florence

An interactive data visualisation showing how 13 million tourists and 370,000 residents share the same city. The numbers tell the story: month by month, zone by zone.

My Role

From raw data to a story

I led the full project from sourcing and cleaning the data, to designing the narrative arc and building the interactive entirely in code.

Sourcing, cleaning and structuring monthly tourism data from ISTAT, Regione Toscana, and CST Firenze
Narrative Design
Structuring 12 months as a scrollytelling arc with a single protagonist: the tourist-to-resident ratio

Problem

The data existed. The story didn't.

Florence's tourism statistics are published every year but in PDF reports written for municipal officials. The numbers answer "how many?" but never "what does it feel like to live here?"

→PDF annual reports city-wide totals, no seasonal or geographic breakdown
→ISTAT tables accurate but unintelligible without human context
→Smart city dashboards designed for administrators, optimised for KPIs, not for empathy
→No single source visualised how the balance shifts month by month and neighbourhood by neighbourhood

Design Approach

Designing for understanding,  not information

The central question wasn't "how do I display this data?" but "how do I make someone feel what it means to live in Florence in July?" Every decision followed from that.

→Dot-grid method. Each dot = 10,000 people, Tourist dots and  Resident dots occupy the same space, making the imbalance visible and immediate
→Scroll as time. Each chapter is one month and the panel updates as you scroll down, mimicking the passage of the year from January to December
→The ratio as protagonist. One large number dominates the panel. Everything else supports it.
→Two zones, one comparison. A visual comparison between Centro Storico and the rest of the city is clear and evident.

What I found:

Context is the data
"988,000 tourists" is a number. "27 for every person who lives here" is a story. The ratio reframes the same figure completely.
Less is more impact
Early versions showed multiple metrics. Every element added reduced the power of the one that mattered. Removing elements was the hardest part.
Crowding is physical
Dots communicate crowding perfectly, bringing the feeling of overcrowding directly to the viewer.
Visual Language

A system built around one constraint

Every visual decision had to earn its place by making the data clearer. Two main colours, one typeface, no chart axes. If it didn't serve the story, it was removed.

Design Principles

Rules that guided every decision

One number, maximum impact
The tourist-to-resident ratio is the single most important piece of information. The entire visual system exists to make that number immediately visible.
Animate the exception
Animation highlights the highest number across the year. Every other month transitions cleanly.
Don't spoil the story
The user won't know the numbers for all months before scrolling to them.
Data structure = narrative structure
The monthly ISTAT data naturally suggested months as the unit of the story. The structure came from the data, not from an idea imposed on top of it.

Reflection

Qualitative outcomes

Effective storytelling is built on clarity, clean interfaces, and letting the user explore the data at their own pace.
The hardest part was finding the right visualisation, one that felt natural.
→The dot-grid communicates crowding better than any chart type because crowding is physical and spatial
→Mobile-first data visualisation requires a completely different layout strategy than desktop
→Narrative and story naturally follow the data — the structure emerged from the dataset, not the other way around
